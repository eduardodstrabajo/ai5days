# Cooking Best Practices (Skill)

Description:
This skill provides concise, cooking best practices to reduce purine concentration and avoid unnecessary acidification in high-cuisine preparations. 

Key Cooking Best Practices

1. Separate stocks and cooking streams
- Use dedicated pots for animal proteins and for high-purine plant ingredients (lentils, dried peas, certain mushrooms, yeast extracts). Never reuse concentrated cooking liquid between these streams.

2. Prefer short-cook methods for proteins
- Use searing, quick poaching or brief roasting for meats and seafood. Finish with lightly diluted vegetable broths rather than concentrating stocks into glazes.

3. Limit long reductions
- Slow, long simmering or concentrated reductions concentrate purines. If reductions are needed for flavor, dilute before finishing or discard the heaviest fraction.

4. Manage acidity and spice pairings
- Inventory acidifiers (vinegar, wine, citrus, tamarind, tomatoes). When avoiding acidity, favor fresh herbs and warm aromatics (ginger, turmeric, cumin). Avoid combining multiple acidifying agents in one cooking liquid.

5. pH measurement & thresholds
- Use pH test strips or a calibrated handheld pH meter to check cooking liquids after simmer and after any reduction step. Operational thresholds for kitchen decision-making:
  - Aim for finishing liquids with pH >= 6.0 when minimizing acidity is desired.
  - If a stock or reduction measures <= 5.5, dilute with fresh vegetable broth or discard the concentrated fraction and remake with a lighter stock.
- Calibrate digital meters daily with standard buffers, rinse probes between samples, and keep strips dry and sealed until use.

6. Strain, dilute, discard
- After using poaching liquids or stocks, strain and discard if there is risk of purine concentration. Use fresh broth for final sauces.

7. Portion control and plating
- Serve smaller portions of high-purine items and pair them with vitamin C–rich vegetables to support uric acid clearance.

8. Documentation
- Document SOPs for stock separation and safe discard; keep recipes annotated for separation requirements.

Caveats
- These are culinary and nutritional strategies based on food chemistry principles and gout dietary guidance. They do not replace medical treatment. For clinical recommendations, consult a registered dietitian or rheumatologist.

Usage
- Use this checklist in kitchen SOPs and recipe notes for low‑purine preparation.
