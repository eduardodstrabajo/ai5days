# Purine Expert Skill

Description:
This skill provides evidence-informed guidance linking dietary purine risk with metabolic contributors such as elevated blood glucose (hyperglycemia) and high blood pressure (hypertension). Use when producing dietary recommendations, evidence summaries, or clinical-facing advice about foods that may indirectly raise serum urate by increasing insulin resistance, glycemic load, or vascular stress.

Key Clinical Note:
- High purine burden is not only driven by purine-rich foods; metabolic conditions like high blood sugar and hypertension modify purine metabolism and renal urate clearance.
- Foods that raise blood glucose (e.g., sugar-sweetened beverages, refined carbohydrates, foods high in added sugars or high-fructose corn syrup) can increase de novo purine synthesis and uric acid production.
- Foods that worsen blood pressure or vascular load (e.g., high-sodium processed foods, large amounts of processed meats, frequent consumption of fried/ultra-processed meals) may impair renal function and urate excretion, indirectly contributing to elevated serum urate.

Practical Guidance / Actions:
- When advising on purine reduction, assess and address glycemic load and sodium intake as co-factors.
- Recommend low-glycemic whole foods: vegetables, whole fruits, legumes, whole grains, lean proteins, and fiber-rich options to blunt post-prandial glucose spikes.
- Advise sodium reduction and limit highly processed, preserved, and fast foods to support blood pressure and renal health.
- Counsel on limiting sugar-sweetened beverages and high-fructose foods; prefer water, unsweetened teas, and tart cherry preparations without added sweeteners.
- Emphasize weight management, increased physical activity, and adequate hydration as adjunct strategies to lower uric acid.

When to Refer / Safety:
- This guidance is supportive and not a replacement for personalized medical care. If users have diabetes, uncontrolled hypertension, chronic kidney disease, or are on urate-lowering therapy, recommend prompt clinical follow-up.

Evidence & Sources:
- PubMed / NCBI reviews on hyperuricemia, metabolic syndrome, and dietary glycemic load
- Mayo Clinic guidance on gout and dietary contributors
- Recent reviews on fructose metabolism and uric acid (health portals and peer-reviewed literature)

Usage Notes for Agents:
- Invoke this skill when generating diet summaries, patient-facing recommendations, or when asked to reconcile purine risk with metabolic comorbidities.
- Produce inline evidence tags (e.g., "(PubMed)", "(Mayo Clinic)") for clinical claims and include a brief safety disclaimer.

Coaching Tone:
Concise, evidence-focused, clinician-aware. Prioritize risk mitigation, simple food swaps, and referrals for clinical evaluation when relevant.
